Think About a Child Executive Summary

I. Executive Summary

Think About a Child Organization is a 501(c)(3) charitable nonprofit organization based in Pottsville, Pennsylvania with a mission to facilitate the long-term sustainability of Haiti. It is our goal to establish a facility in the south of, Haiti to provide multiple programs such as shelter, education, nutrition, worship, healthcare, and agricultural needs of our target population throughout the country. Our organization strives to lead by example creating a united force that fosters a climate of growth and personal development for its inhabitants.

Implemented by a committed and well-trained staff, our program will provide many opportunities for the reduction of poverty and disadvantage through various interventions. Supporting the development of knowledge, health, values and self-sufficiency, Think About a Child Organization will provide a thorough foundation for future success to many deprived and underprivileged individuals.

Initiated by Pierre Jeune, our organization aims to create positive, life-changing experiences for the target population in Haiti and so that it can achieve their true potential. Individual empowerment and self-determination are strong components of the Think About a Child Organization. Established in 2017, our organization was created and organized to address the basic needs and more of some of the world’s most disadvantaged individuals, to counteract indigence, and to ameliorate the quality of life of the severely underserved population in Haiti.

Mr. Pierre Jeune and our Board of Directors are aware of the needs throughout Haiti and will provide services that cater to its people. Our organization’s goals are accomplished by the implementation of activities and materials that are focused on a well-developed prospectus. Ongoing evaluation assures progress and cost-effectiveness.

III. Problem Statement

Haiti, the poorest country in the Western hemisphere, is plagued by violence, hunger, unrelenting extreme poverty, disease, high unemployment rates, low life expectancy and crumbling health and educational systems. Haiti is a beautiful country but even the beautiful sunsets and green mountains can not hide the poverty. Most families live in crowed homes made of cardboard and tin.

There is no running water and little or no electricity. Urban slums teem with garbage, sewage and people. There is never enough food. Women and children are always hungry. Illness and disease are common, especially among children, as more than half the population has no access to Western medicine. Unemployment ranges from 60% to 70%; those with jobs earn a minimum wage of just $1.50 a day.

The education system is partly to blame for some of the problems facing Haitians. The schools were established shortly after the Constitution of 1805, which mandated free and compulsory primary education. The Education Act of 1848 created rural primary schools with a limited, mostly agricultural curriculum. While education was promoted, accessible school system never developed because of the post-colonial leaders.

Today, the majority received no formal education and only a small minority are educated beyond primary school. In recent years, there have been efforts to reform the educational system and to make education more accessible to the poor and more relevant to their needs. The reforms, however, have been only partly successful at best and much of the old system remains in place throughout the country. Despite the reforms, obtaining an education remains an elusive goal for most in Haiti. Although education is highly valued, the majority do not have access to it. The enrollment rate for primary school is 67%, of which less than 30% reach 6th grade. Secondary schools enroll 20% of eligible-age children. The literacy rate is about 53% falls well below the 90% average literacy rate for Latin American and Caribbean countries. .

Even though education is technically free in Haiti, it remains beyond the means of most Haitians, who cannot afford the supplemental fees, school supplies, and uniforms required. Reform measures, especially the use of Haitian Creole, have met resistance. Thus, education remains a privilege of the upper and middle classes, with fluency in French a marker of success.
